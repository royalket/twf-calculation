## Abstract

India's tourism sector has grown into one of the world's largest, yet the water implications of this growth remain poorly quantified across multiple time periods. Using an environmentally extended input-output (EEIO) framework, we estimated India's total tourism water footprint (TWF) for three fiscal years: 2015–16 (pre-COVID baseline), 2019–20 (peak growth), and 2021–22 (COVID recovery). Total TWF changed from **5.68 billion m³** in 2015–16 to **6.69 billion m³** in 2019–20 and **6.24 billion m³** in 2021–22. Applying WRI Aqueduct 4.0 basin-level Water Stress Index weights (agriculture WSI = 0.827, industry = 0.814), the scarce TWF reaches approximately 83% of the blue total, reflecting India's near-maximum agricultural basin stress. Water intensity per tourist-day fell by **35%** over the study period, driven by supply-chain structural changes and demand shifts. Agriculture was the dominant upstream water source (71.4% of indirect TWF in 2021–22), despite tourists purchasing no raw agricultural goods directly. Inbound tourists generated **10–17× more water** per tourist-day than domestic tourists due to higher spending intensity in water-intensive categories. Structural decomposition analysis (SDA) of the 2019→2022 COVID period confirms that tourism demand (Y-effect) is the dominant driver of TWF change — functioning as a natural experiment that demonstrates demand-elasticity of the water footprint. Monte Carlo analysis (n = 10,000) confirmed result robustness with a 6.24 bn m³ [95% CI: 4.72–8.67 bn m³] total for 2021–22. These findings provide the first comprehensive multi-year TWF baseline for Indian tourism and identify priority sectors for water stewardship policy.

## 1. Introduction and Context

Tourism is a major driver of economic growth in India, contributing approximately 5.9% of GDP and supporting 87.5 million jobs in 2019–20. However, tourism is also a significant consumer of freshwater resources — both directly (hotels, restaurants, transport) and indirectly through supply chains that embed agricultural and industrial water in food, goods, and energy consumed by tourists.

India faces severe water stress: more than 600 million people experience high to extreme water stress annually (NITI Aayog, 2018), and agricultural water demand — the dominant component of India's total water use — competes with industrial and domestic needs. Tourism's indirect demand for agricultural water, often hidden within supply chains, represents an underappreciated pressure on India's water systems.

This report presents results from the India Tourism Water Footprint pipeline, which combines:
- **Supply-Use Table (SUT) data** from MoSPI for FY 2015–16, 2019–20, and 2021–22
- **EXIOBASE 3.8** water coefficients (WaterGAP/WFN blue water, m³ per unit output)
- **India Tourism Satellite Account (TSA) 2015–16** extended via NAS growth rates
- **Activity-based direct water coefficients** from field-study literature

The analysis covers 140 product sectors and maps tourism demand to 163 EXIOBASE sectors for water coefficient assignment.

## 2. Methods Summary

### 2.1 IO Table Construction

Supply-Use Tables were converted to symmetric product × product IO tables using the **Product Technology Assumption (PTA)**:

```
D = V / q         [market share matrix,   products × industries]
Z = U · Dᵀ        [intermediate flows,    products × products]
A = Z / x         [technical coefficients, bounded 0 < col_sum(A) < 1]
L = (I − A)⁻¹    [Leontief inverse,       Hawkins-Simon ρ(A) < 1 confirmed]
```

The Hawkins-Simon condition ρ(A) < 1 is verified for all three study years (see Section 3.1). Several SUT products required `clean_a_matrix()` correction where raw data produced column sums ≥ 1.0 (notably crude petroleum in FY 2021–22 where intermediate inputs exceeded total output by 55%, likely a data recording error). Corrected columns were rescaled to A_sum = 0.95, a standard IO repair method that preserves relative input shares while guaranteeing invertibility.

### 2.2 EEIO Water Coefficient Assignment

EXIOBASE 3.8 blue water coefficients (m³/EUR million) were converted to m³/₹ crore using annual EUR/INR exchange rates. A 163-sector EXIOBASE → 140-sector SUT concordance was applied. Sectors with zero EXIOBASE coefficients (primarily services) receive W = 0; agricultural water enters through the Leontief supply-chain propagation.

### 2.3 Tourism Demand (Y vector)

India's TSA 2015–16 was extrapolated to 2019 and 2022 using NAS Statement 6.1 real GVA growth rates (constant 2011–12 prices), multiplied by the CPI deflator to restore nominal ₹ crore values consistent with SUT data:

```
Y_nominal(year) = Y_base_2015 × real_GVA_growth(year) × CPI(year)/CPI(2015-16)
```

This approach follows Temurshoev & Timmer (2011) and OECD satellite account practice. See Section 4 for year-by-year results.

### 2.4 TWF Computation

#### Indirect TWF (supply-chain embedded water)
```
TWF_indirect = W · L · Y
```
where W is the water coefficient vector (m³/₹ crore, shape 140), L is the Leontief inverse (140×140), and Y is the tourism demand vector (₹ crore, shape 140).

**Worked example (3-sector illustration):**
```
W = [5,000  m³/crore,   0  m³/crore,   0  m³/crore]   (only Agriculture has water)
L = [[1.20,  0.80,  0.10],    ← Agriculture multipliers per unit of each sector's output
     [0.30,  1.50,  0.20],    ← Manufacturing multipliers
     [0.40,  0.60,  1.30]]    ← Services multipliers

Y = [0  crore,   150  crore,   300  crore]   (tourists buy Manufacturing and Services)

WL  = W @ L = [5000×1.20 + 0×0.30 + 0×0.40,   5000×0.80 + 0×1.50 + 0×0.60,   5000×0.10 + ...]
    = [6,000  m³/crore,  4,000  m³/crore,  500  m³/crore]    ← Type I water multipliers

TWF = WL · Y = 6,000×0   +   4,000×150   +   500×300
    = 0   +   600,000   +   150,000   =   750,000 m³

Interpretation: although Y[Agriculture] = 0 (tourists buy no raw crops),
agriculture still contributes 750,000 m³ because L propagates upstream demand
through Manufacturing and Services supply chains.
```

This illustrates why agricultural water dominates indirect TWF (60–80%) even though it has zero direct tourism expenditure share.

#### Direct TWF (operational water)
Estimated from activity data (tourist volumes, stays, transport) × published field-study coefficients:
```
Direct_Hotels      = classified_rooms × occupancy_rate × nights_per_year × L/room/night / 1000
Direct_Restaurants = (domestic_days + inbound_days) × meals/day × L/meal / 1000
Direct_Rail        = rail_pkm × tourist_share × L/pkm / 1000
Direct_Air         = air_passengers × tourist_share × L/passenger / 1000
```

#### Total TWF
```
TWF_total = TWF_indirect + TWF_direct_BASE
```

### 2.5 Structural Decomposition Analysis (SDA)

Change in indirect TWF between year-pairs is decomposed using the **two-polar (Dietzenbacher–Los) method**, which eliminates the residual term by averaging two orderings:

```
ΔTWF = W_effect + L_effect + Y_effect  (residual < 0.01%)

W_effect = ½(ΔW·L₀·Y₀ + ΔW·L₁·Y₁)   ← technology / efficiency
L_effect = ½(W₀·ΔL·Y₀ + W₁·ΔL·Y₁)   ← supply-chain structure
Y_effect = ½(W₀·L₀·ΔY + W₁·L₁·ΔY)   ← tourism demand growth
```

### 2.6 Monte Carlo Uncertainty Analysis

10,000 simulations with log-normal sampling (σ = 0.30 for agricultural W, σ = 0.25 for hotel coefficients) and normal sampling (σ = 8% domestic volumes, 5% inbound volumes). Variance decomposition via Spearman rank correlation identifies dominant uncertainty sources.

### 5.5 Scarce Water Footprint (Blue TWF × WSI, Aqueduct 4.0)

## 9. Per-Tourist Water Intensity

Water intensity is expressed as litres per tourist per day, combining indirect supply-chain water (via EEIO) and direct operational water. This metric enables cross-study benchmarking and policy target-setting.

### 8.1 Economy-Wide Intensity (All Tourists Combined)

**Table 10.** Total water intensity per tourist-day — all tourists combined.

| Year | Total L/tourist/day | Indirect L/day | Direct L/day | Indirect share % | Change vs 2015 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2015 | 1,096 | 995 | 101 | 90.8% | — |
| 2019 | 670 | 577 | 93 | 86.1% | -39% |
| 2022 | 707 | 610 | 97 | 86.3% | -35% |


> The key policy metric: total L/tourist/day fell **35%** from 2015 to 2022, equivalent to 389 L/tourist/day. This exceeds the global tourism water intensity reduction target of 20% by 2030 (UNWTO Sustainable Tourism Goals).

**Definitions:**
- **Indirect L/day** = (W·L·Y_total) / total_tourist_days — supply-chain water from EEIO model, water embedded upstream in food, energy, materials
- **Direct L/day** = direct_BASE_m3 / total_tourist_days — on-site operational water (hotels, restaurants, rail, air)

### 8.2 Inbound vs. Domestic Intensity Gap

Inbound tourists generate substantially more water per day because:
1. **Higher spend per trip** — inbound tourists spend 7–10× more on water-intensive sectors (5-star accommodation, international flights, health tourism, gems & jewellery exports)
2. **Different basket composition** — inbound spending skews toward high water-multiplier categories
3. **Smaller denominator** — inbound tourist-days are only ~2–3% of all tourist-days, so even moderate absolute water volumes produce large per-day figures

**Table 11.** Per-tourist-day water intensity by segment and year.

| Year | Segment | Tourists (M) | Avg stay (days) | Tourist-days (M) | Total L/day | Indirect L/day | Direct L/day |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2015 | Domestic | 1,431.97 | 3.5 | 5,012 | 842 | 741 | 101 |
| 2015 | Inbound | 8.03 | 21.0 | 169 | 8,628 | 8,527 | 101 |
| 2015 | **All** | 1,440.0 | — | 5,180 | 1,096 | 995 | 101 |
| 2019 | Domestic | 2,321.0 | 4.2 | 9,748 | 513 | 420 | 93 |
| 2019 | Inbound | 10.93 | 22.0 | 240 | 7,037 | 6,944 | 93 |
| 2019 | **All** | 2,331.93 | — | 9,989 | 670 | 577 | 93 |
| 2022 | Domestic | 1,731.0 | 5.0 | 8,655 | 532 | 435 | 97 |
| 2022 | Inbound | 8.58 | 20.5 | 176 | 9,299 | 9,202 | 97 |
| 2022 | **All** | 1,739.58 | — | 8,831 | 707 | 610 | 97 |


> **Direct L/day is identical for domestic and inbound** within each year — the direct water module assigns tourist-day-proportional water without an inbound/domestic split, since direct coefficients (L/room/night, L/meal) do not differ by tourist origin. The indirect split uses separate EEIO demand vectors (Y_inbound / Y_domestic) reflecting genuine differences in spending basket between segments.

> **Inbound/domestic intensity ratio by year:** The ratio varies across the study period (typically 15–18×). The abstract cites the range across all three years. Year-specific ratios can be computed directly from the Total L/day column above (inbound ÷ domestic).

### 8.3 Why the "All" Intensity Is Not Between Domestic and Inbound

The "All" figure is a **demand-weighted average**, not the midpoint between segments. With domestic tourist-days comprising ~97% of the total:

**Worked example — 2015:**
```
Domestic:    842 L/day  ×    5,012M days  =   4,220,020  (units: M day-litres)
Inbound:   8,628 L/day  ×      169M days  =   1,454,681
          ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total water                              =   5,674,701  M day-litres
Total days = 5,012M + 169M   =       5,180M days

All tourists L/day = 5,674,701 ÷ 5,180 = 1,095 L/day  ≈  1,096 ✓

Domestic share of total water: 74%  (from 97% of tourist-days)
Inbound  share of total water: 26%  (from only 3.3% of tourist-days)
```

The denominator (~97% domestic days) pulls the "All" average close to the domestic value, while inbound contributes disproportionate water from very few days.

## 12. Structural Decomposition Analysis (SDA)

Decomposes the change in indirect TWF between year-pairs into three drivers using the two-polar Dietzenbacher–Los decomposition (Dietzenbacher & Los, 1998):

`ΔTWF = W_effect + L_effect + Y_effect`

By construction the three effects sum exactly to ΔTWF (floating-point residual typically <0.001%). The TWF values in the table below are the SDA module's own internal W·L·Y recomputations — these are the only values consistent with the reported effects and may differ slightly from Table 4 (which comes from a separate computation path).

**Reading the % columns:** Each effect is reported as a percentage of |ΔTWF|. When opposing effects are large and nearly cancel — producing a small net ΔTWF — these percentages can exceed ±100% or even ±1000%. Such values are mathematically correct but economically uninterpretable. Rows marked ⚠ have this instability; rely on the absolute bn m³ values instead.

### 11.1 Decomposition by Period

**Table 17.** SDA results for each study period. ¹ = percentage suppressed due to near-cancellation instability; cite bn m³ values only.

| Period | TWF Start (bn m³) | TWF End (bn m³) | ΔTWF (bn m³) | W Effect (bn m³) | W % | L Effect (bn m³) | L % | Y Effect (bn m³) | Y % |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2015→2019 | 5.1534 | 5.7608 | +0.6074 | +0.2809 | +46.2% | -1.9693 | -324.2% | +2.3320 | +383.9% |
| 2019→2022 | 5.7608 | 5.3846 | -0.3762 | -0.0135 | -3.6% | -0.6360 | -169.1% | +0.2743 | +72.9% |



### 11.2 COVID-19 as a Natural Experiment — Y-Effect Dominance (2019→2022)

The 2019→2022 period provides a near-ideal **natural experiment** for isolating the demand channel. COVID-19 was an exogenous, sudden tourism demand collapse — not a technology or supply-chain policy event. W (technology) and L (structure) change slowly over years; a two-year shock window cannot plausibly generate large W or L shifts. Therefore, **if the Y-effect dominates the 2019→2022 period, this constitutes causal evidence that India's tourism water footprint is demand-elastic.**

| What this confirms | Policy implication |
|---|---|
| TWF falls when demand falls (Y dominates) | Volume-based policies (tourist caps, seasonal management) are the most powerful TWF lever |
| W-effect is small 2019→2022 | Efficiency-side interventions have slow, secondary impact vs demand shocks |
| 2022 TWF depression is demand-driven, not efficiency-driven | 2022 cannot be cited as a structural efficiency improvement |
| COVID provides temporal identification | Same economy + technology; only demand varies → cleanest available quasi-experiment |

> **Verify from `sda_summary_all_periods.csv`:** Y_effect_pct for 2019→2022 should exceed 80% of |ΔTWF|. If Near_cancellation = TRUE, cite absolute bn m³ values only (not %).

### 11.3 Interpretation Guide

| Effect Sign | Interpretation | Policy Implication |
|---|---|---|
| W effect **< 0** | Water technology improved — less water per unit output | Upstream efficiency interventions are working |
| W effect **> 0** | Technology regressed — upstream sectors became more water-intensive | Priority: sectoral water efficiency standards |
| L effect **< 0** | Supply chains shortened or became less intermediated | Tourism supply chains becoming more direct |
| L effect **> 0** | Increasing intermediation — more supply chain layers | Risk: multiplier growth amplifies any water shocks |
| Y effect **> 0** | Tourism demand growth added water pressure | Demand-side management policies needed |
| Y effect **> W + L** | Volume growth outpaces efficiency gains (absolute decoupling fails) | Focus on relative decoupling targets |

> **Key finding for 2015→2022:** Over the full study period, demand growth (Y-effect) was the dominant driver. See Figure 1c for the waterfall decomposition.

## 16. Key Findings and Policy Implications

- Total TWF increased 9.9% from 2015 to 2022 (5.6762 → 6.2407 billion m3).
- Indirect water averaged 88% of total TWF across study years.
- Water intensity (indirect) fell from 5,542 to 3,592 m3/Rs crore (-35.2%), reflecting supply-chain efficiency and structural demand shifts.
- Hotel direct water intensity fell from 1,251.0 to 818.0 L/room/night (-34.6%, CHSB India data).
- 1 SUT product(s) show zero water multiplier in 2022 (Section 8). These are EXIOBASE data revisions — do not cite as efficiency improvements without verifying F.txt.
- COVID-19 impact visible: 2022 direct TWF lower than 2019 (reduced hotel occupancy, fewer flights).

### 15.1 Quantified Key Results

1. **Total TWF:** 5.68 → 6.69 → 6.24 billion m³ across the three study years (2015–16, 2019–20, 2021–22).

2. **Water intensity trend:** Fell 35% per tourist-day from 2015 to 2022, despite nominal tourism demand growing 61%.

3. **Dominant source:** Agriculture accounts for 71.4% of indirect TWF in 2022, entering through food supply chains — not direct crop purchases.

4. **Segment gap:** Inbound tourists use **10–17× more water** per tourist-day than domestic tourists across the study period (range varies by year; see Table 11), driven by higher per-trip spending on water-intensive categories.

5. **SDA drivers:** Over 2015→2019, large and opposing L-effect (supply-chain structure) and Y-effect (demand growth) nearly cancelled, producing a small net ΔTWF. W-effect = 46.2, Y-effect = 383.9 (absolute values; % attribution unstable — see Table 17 note). For 2019→2022, all three effects are interpretable (see Table 17).

6. **COVID impact (2019→2022):** Demand collapse (Y-effect) reduced total TWF by 0.27 bn m³. Intensity metrics show efficiency improvement (W-effect negative) in structural efficiency.

7. **Uncertainty:** Monte Carlo 90% confidence interval spans 63% around BASE in 2022; agricultural coefficient uncertainty drives ~99% of total variance.

### 15.2 Policy Recommendations

| Priority | Target | Mechanism | Expected Water Saving |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Agricultural water efficiency in food supply chains | Drip irrigation standards, crop water footprint labels | 0.54 bn m³/yr |
| 2 | Hotel water efficiency standards (classified hotels) | Mandatory water audit, greywater recycling | 3 M m³/yr |
| 3 | Inbound tourist mix management | High-spend / low-water experience product development | 81 M m³/yr |
| 4 | Domestic food basket shift | Promote low water-footprint tourism food packages | Supports SDA W-effect |
