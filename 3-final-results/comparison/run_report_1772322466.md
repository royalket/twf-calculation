### 6B. Inbound vs domestic intensity gap

Inbound tourists generate substantially more water per day than domestic tourists because:
1. **Higher spend per trip** — inbound tourists spend 7–10× more on water-intensive
   sectors (5-star hotels, international flights, health tourism, gems & jewellery exports).
2. **Smaller denominator** — inbound tourist-days are only 2–3% of all tourist-days
   (2.0% in 2022), so even a modest water total produces a
   large per-day figure.

### Why the "All" row is not between Domestic and Inbound

The "All" figure is a **demand-weighted average**, not a simple average of the two segment values.
It is computed as `total_water_m3 / total_tourist_days`, where domestic days dominate the
denominator (~97% of all tourist-days). Using 2015 as an example:

```
Domestic:    922 L/day  ×    5,012M days  =   4,620,972  (units: M day-litres)
Inbound:  13,768 L/day  ×      169M days  =   2,321,285
          ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total water                              =   6,942,257  M day-litres
Total days = 5,012M + 169M   =       5,180M days

All tourists L/day = 6,942,257 ÷ 5,180 = 1,340 L/day  ≈  1,348 ✓

Domestic share of total water: 67%  (from 97% of tourist-days)
Inbound  share of total water: 33%  (from only 3.3% of tourist-days)
```

Domestic contributes the majority of total water despite dominating tourist-days (because per-day
use is low). Inbound contributes a disproportionate share of total water from very few days
(because per-day use is high). Since the denominator is ~97% domestic, the "All" figure is
pulled close to the domestic value — not midway toward the inbound value.

## 10. Structural Decomposition Analysis (SDA)

Decomposes the change in indirect TWF between year-pairs into three drivers
using the two-polar decomposition method (eliminates residual term):

- **W effect** — change in water coefficients (technology / efficiency improvement)
- **L effect** — change in Leontief inverse (supply-chain structure / intermediation)
- **Y effect** — change in tourism demand (volume growth + composition shift)

`ΔTWF = W_effect + L_effect + Y_effect`  (residual < 0.01% confirms decomposition identity)

### 10.1 Decomposition by period

| Period | TWF Start (bn m³) | TWF End (bn m³) | ΔTWF (bn m³) | W Effect (bn m³) | W % | L Effect (bn m³) | L % | Y Effect (bn m³) | Y % |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2015→2019 | 47.8830 | 76.8209 | +28.9378 | +2.1614 | +7.5% | -1.2871 | -4.4% | +28.0406 | +96.9% |
| 2019→2022 | 76.8209 | 75.6348 | -1.1860 | -3.7196 | -313.6% | -0.7877 | -66.4% | +3.3321 | +280.9% |


### 10.2 Interpretation

- A **negative W effect** means water technology improved (less water per unit output).
- A **negative L effect** means supply chains became less water-intensive.
- A **positive Y effect** means tourism demand growth added water pressure.

If Y effect dominates, policy priority is demand-side management (tourist volumes, product mix).
If W effect is small or positive, efficiency interventions in upstream sectors are needed.
